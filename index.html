What is Depression?

According to the World Health Organization, Depression is a common mental disorder. Globally, more than 264 million people of all ages suffer from depression. It is a complex mental disorder which affects the human mind. The mental disorder can affect someone physically, emotionally/mentally, and socially, and have an impactful effect on someone’s daily life.

From the Ministry of Health in New Zealand, causes of depression vary, but possible factors are biological, psychological, and/or social sources of pressure, and could lead to changes in the brain’s function and behavior negatively. Pessimistic emotions and symptoms can last for weeks or even months.

Symptoms include:
  • feeling tired all the time
  • getting too much sleep or not enough
  • feeling worthless and helpless
  • thinking about death a lot
  • having no energy and feelings of low self-esteem
  • loss of appetite or overeating
  • sadness or emotional ‘numbness’
  • loss of pleasure in everyday activities
  • irritability or anxiety
  • poor concentration
  • feeling guilt or crying for no apparent reason.
  • Change in weight

A New Zealand Health survey was done in 2013, and it revealed that 14.3% of New Zealand adults had been diagnosed with Clinical Depression at some point in their lives. That is more than half a million people who had been struggling with this mental illness.

The Misunderstanding

It’s difficult to cover the true meaning of the mental illness, so many people believe or spread beliefs and opinions of what Depression is.
One of the most debated beliefs is that “it is not a real illness; it’s just a phase”. Most people believe that this disorder is just a phase in life; that it’s just a mild issue in your head.
Depression can also be associated with the opinion that it’s just being sad/upset all the time, and you can “snap out of it”. It is more seen as emotional change rather than brain function, chemistry, and structure change.
